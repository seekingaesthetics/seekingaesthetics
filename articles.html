How Many Units of Botox Do You Actually Need?

Seeking Aesthetics · Utah · 2026 · 5 min read
For informational purposes only. This article does not constitute medical advice. Always consult a licensed medical professional before pursuing any aesthetic treatment.

It's the question every first-timer asks — and most providers won't answer until you're already in the chair. Here's the honest breakdown of Botox units by treatment area, what baby Botox actually means, and why the number matters more than the price per unit.

Why Units Matter More Than Price Per Unit

In Utah Valley, Botox runs $10–$15 per unit in 2026. That range sounds manageable — until a provider uses 40 units when 20 would have done the job. The price per unit is only half the equation. The number of units your injector uses is where the real cost lives.

Units By Treatment Area

Forehead lines: 10–30 units. The wide range reflects muscle strength — men typically need more, and first-timers often start conservative. Glabella (the 11s): 15–25 units — one of the most common first treatments. Crow's feet: 6–15 units per side. Baby Botox (preventative): 20–40 units total, placed lightly across multiple areas.

What Baby Botox Actually Means

Preventative Botox — sometimes called baby Botox — uses fewer units placed more lightly to soften expression lines before they become permanent. It's increasingly popular in Utah Valley among patients in their mid-to-late 20s. The goal isn't frozen. It's preserved.

The Red Flag to Watch For

A good injector tells you exactly how many units they're placing before they start — and explains why. If your provider won't give you a unit count upfront, that's a red flag. Morpheus8 vs. Sylfirm X for Acne Scars: Which Is Right for You?

Seeking Aesthetics · Utah · 2026 · 6 min read
For informational purposes only. This article does not constitute medical advice. Always consult a licensed medical professional before pursuing any aesthetic treatment.

RF microneedling is the fastest-growing treatment category in Utah Valley right now. Both Morpheus8 and Sylfirm X use radiofrequency energy — but they work differently and are right for different skin concerns.

What They Have in Common

Both treatments use microneedles to deliver radiofrequency energy into the deeper layers of skin, stimulating collagen production. Both treat acne scars, skin texture, and fine lines. Both require a series of treatments for best results — typically 3 sessions spaced 4–6 weeks apart.

Where They Differ

Morpheus8 uses bipolar RF delivered at a deeper depth — up to 4mm — making it particularly effective for skin laxity and fat remodeling. It's the go-to for patients dealing with skin looseness after weight loss or GLP-1 medication. Sylfirm X uses pulsed wave RF technology specifically designed to target the basement membrane of the skin — the layer responsible for redness, rosacea, and vascular irregularities. It's newer, has less downtime, and is the stronger choice specifically for acne scarring and redness.

The Verdict for Acne Scars

If acne scarring is your primary concern — especially with underlying redness or discoloration — Sylfirm X has the edge. If your scars are deeper and textural with skin laxity, Morpheus8 may be the stronger choice. Many Utah Valley providers now offer both — ask specifically which one they recommend for your skin type and why.

GLP-1 & Your Skin: What Ozempic Patients Need to Know

Seeking Aesthetics · Utah · 2026 · 5 min read
For informational purposes only. This article does not constitute medical advice. Always consult a licensed medical professional before pursuing any aesthetic treatment.

GLP-1 medications like Ozempic and Wegovy have changed the weight loss landscape — but they've also created a new wave of patients dealing with something nobody warned them about. Rapid weight loss, especially in the face, can cause significant volume loss and skin laxity that diet and exercise alone can't address.

What GLP-1 Does to Your Face

The term "Ozempic face" has become common — and it's real. As the body loses weight rapidly, facial fat pads deflate. Cheeks hollow. The under-eye area deepens. Skin that was stretched around volume now lacks support. The skin itself didn't age — it lost the structure underneath it.

What Actually Helps

Dermal fillers — particularly Sculptra and Radiesse — can restore volume lost during rapid weight loss. Sculptra works by stimulating your own collagen over several months, making it especially well-suited for GLP-1 patients who want gradual, natural-looking results. For skin laxity, Morpheus8 or other RF microneedling treatments can tighten the skin itself rather than just filling beneath it.

What to Ask Your Provider

Before your appointment ask: Do you have experience treating GLP-1 patients specifically? What combination approach do you recommend for my level of volume loss? Are you using Sculptra, Radiesse, or hyaluronic acid fillers — and why? A good provider will not just sell you the filler — they'll explain why one product makes more sense than another for your specific situation.

Filler vs. Botox: Which One Is Actually Right for You?

Seeking Aesthetics · Utah · 2026 · 4 min read
For informational purposes only. This article does not constitute medical advice. Always consult a licensed medical professional before pursuing any aesthetic treatment.

Botox and filler are the two most common aesthetic treatments in Utah Valley — and the two most commonly confused. Patients often come in asking for one when they actually need the other. Here's the honest breakdown.

What Botox Does

Botox (and Dysport, Xeomin, Daxxify) are neuromodulators. They temporarily relax specific muscles to reduce the appearance of lines caused by movement — forehead lines, the 11s, crow's feet. Botox does not add volume. It simply prevents the muscle from contracting as forcefully. Results typically last 3–4 months.

What Filler Does

Dermal fillers add volume. Most fillers in Utah Valley are hyaluronic acid-based (Juvederm, Restylane, RHA Collection). Filler is used to restore volume lost with age, enhance features like lips and cheeks, and soften deep folds that Botox can't address. Results typically last 9–18 months depending on the product and area.

How to Know Which One You Need

Simple rule: if the line or concern is present even when your face is completely relaxed, Botox alone won't fix it. Static lines — the ones you see at rest — typically need filler or a combination approach. Dynamic lines — the ones that appear when you smile or raise your brows — respond well to Botox. A qualified injector will assess your face at rest and in motion before recommending anything.

Utah Valley has one of the highest MedSpa densities in the country — which means pricing competition is fierce. $9/unit Botox deals are real. But the question isn't whether the price is low. The question is what you're actually getting for it.

What Credentials Actually Mean

In Utah, injectable treatments can legally be performed by a range of licensed professionals. The license type is just the starting point. What matters is how many injections that person has performed, in what settings, on what types of patients, and under what level of supervision. A newly licensed RN with six months of experience is technically qualified — but they are not the same as a nurse practitioner with 2,000 Botox treatments over six years.

The Questions to Ask Before You Book

How many years have you been injecting specifically? How many treatments do you perform per week? What is your approach when a patient is unhappy with results? Do you have before and after photos of patients with a similar concern — with treatment details attached? A confident, experienced injector will answer these without hesitation.

Why Transparent Pricing Tells You Something

A provider who lists their prices openly — per unit, per syringe, with bundle offers explained — is a provider who respects their patients enough to have that conversation upfront. Providers who hide their pricing behind 'call for a consultation' are often hiding something else too. Price transparency is a proxy for practice integrity.

What Is a MedSpa — and How Is It Different From a Day Spa?

Seeking Aesthetics · Utah · 2026 · 4 min read
For informational purposes only. This article does not constitute medical advice. Always consult a licensed medical professional before pursuing any aesthetic treatment. Seeking Aesthetics is not a healthcare provider.

The difference between a MedSpa and a day spa isn't about how nice the lobby looks. It's about who's legally allowed to perform your treatment — and whether the person holding the needle is licensed to do it.

The Core Difference: Medical Oversight

A day spa is staffed by licensed estheticians and massage therapists. They can perform facials, massages, and basic skincare treatments. What they cannot do: administer injectables, perform laser treatments, or use devices that penetrate below the skin's surface. Those require a licensed medical professional.1

A MedSpa — medical spa — operates under the supervision of a licensed physician, nurse practitioner, or physician assistant. According to the American Med Spa Association, a MedSpa must include a licensed healthcare professional overseeing all clinical treatments.2 This oversight is what makes Botox, fillers, laser resurfacing, and other treatments legally possible.

What Each Can and Cannot Offer

Why This Matters for Your Safety

Botox is a prescription medication. Fillers are injected into living tissue. Lasers operate as FDA Class II or III medical devices. None of these can legally be performed outside a medically supervised setting.3 When you're at a day spa and someone offers you an injectable, that's a red flag — not a deal.

What to Ask Before You Book

Before booking at any MedSpa: Who is the medical director? What is their license type (MD, NP, PA)? Is the person performing your treatment licensed — and with what license? What Is DOPL?

DOPL stands for the Utah Division of Professional Licensing — a division of the Utah Department of Commerce that issues and regulates professional licenses across all healthcare fields.1 If a provider is legally licensed to inject in Utah, their name and license status appear in the DOPL database.

How to Check in 60 Seconds

Step 1: Go to secure.utah.gov/llv/search
Step 2: Enter the provider's name or license number
Step 3: Confirm the license is Active, the license type is correct (RN, NP, APRN, PA, or MD), and the expiration date is current

A legitimate provider will give you their license number without hesitation. If they won't — that's your answer.

Lip Filler: What to Expect Before, During, and After

Seeking Aesthetics · Utah · 2026 · 5 min read
For informational purposes only. This article does not constitute medical advice. Always consult a licensed medical professional before pursuing any aesthetic treatment. Seeking Aesthetics is not a healthcare provider.

Lip filler is one of the most requested aesthetic treatments in Utah — and one of the most feared by first-timers. The swelling, the duck-lip concern, the bruising. Here's the honest, day-by-day breakdown of what actually happens.

What Goes In

Most lip fillers use hyaluronic acid (HA) — a substance naturally found in the body. FDA-approved options include Juvederm Ultra XC, Juvederm Volbella, Restylane Kysse, and Restylane Silk. HA fillers are preferred because they're reversible with hyaluronidase if the result isn't right.1

The Day-by-Day Recovery Timeline

DayWhat You'll SeeIs It Normal?
Day 0 (day of)Significant swelling, possible bruising, overfilled appearance✓ Yes
Days 1–3Swelling peaks, lips look larger than final result✓ Yes
Days 4–5Swelling begins to reduce, lips look more balanced✓ Yes

What to Avoid After Treatment

Avoid for 24–48 hours: Direct sun exposure, intense exercise, alcohol, kissing, using straws, touching or massaging the lips, sleeping face-down. Avoid for 2 weeks: Hot tubs, saunas, extreme heat, and any other facial treatments in the area.2

How Long Does It Last?

Most patients see results lasting 6–12 months, depending on the HA product used, how much was injected, and individual metabolism. Juvederm Volbella tends to last longer than Ultra XC. Most patients return every 9–12 months for a refresh.

The Biggest Mistake First-Timers Make

Booking too close to an event. If you're getting lip filler before a wedding, vacation, or photo shoot — book at least 2 weeks in advance. Your final result won't be visible until the swelling clears. Plan accordingly.

Botox vs. Dysport: What's Actually Different?

Seeking Aesthetics · Utah · 2026 · 4 min read
For informational purposes only. This article does not constitute medical advice. Always consult a licensed medical professional before pursuing any aesthetic treatment. Seeking Aesthetics is not a healthcare provider.

Both Botox and Dysport are botulinum toxin type A. Both relax facial muscles to reduce wrinkles. So why do experienced injectors often prefer one over the other for specific areas? The difference is real — and it affects your results.

The Side-by-Side Comparison

What Faster Onset Actually Means

Dysport is often reported to show results 1–2 days faster than Botox.1 For most patients, this difference is irrelevant — both reach full effect at 14 days. But if you have an event in 7–10 days and had to choose, Dysport's faster onset could be a meaningful advantage.

The Unit Difference — and Why It Matters for Price

Dysport appears cheaper per unit ($4–8 vs $12–20 for Botox), but you need approximately 2.5x more Dysport units to achieve the same effect. Clinical studies consistently use a 2.5:1 dosage ratio.2 When comparing quotes, always compare total treatment cost — not per-unit price.

Which Is Better?

For most patients, either works well. The best choice depends on your treatment area, your provider's preference and experience, and how your body has responded in the past. An experienced injector will recommend based on your anatomy — not based on which product is on sale.

Is Morpheus8 Worth It? An Honest Look at the Most Talked-About Device

Seeking Aesthetics · Utah · 2026 · 5 min read
For informational purposes only. This article does not constitute medical advice. Always consult a licensed medical professional before pursuing any aesthetic treatment. Seeking Aesthetics is not a healthcare provider.

Morpheus8 is everywhere on aesthetic social media — and the before-and-afters are compelling. But at $600–$4,000 per session, is it actually worth it? Here's what the clinical evidence says, who it works best for, and what no one tells you before you book.

What Morpheus8 Actually Is

Morpheus8 is an FDA-cleared radiofrequency microneedling device that combines two technologies: microneedles that create microscopic channels in the skin, and radiofrequency energy delivered through those needles to heat tissue at depths up to 8mm.1 That depth is what separates it from standard microneedling — most competing devices reach 2–3.5mm.

What the Research Shows

A 2022 review published in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology (PubMed PMID: 35916259) confirmed Morpheus8's efficacy for facial rejuvenation, acne scarring, rosacea, and melasma. A multicenter study of 247 patients showed measurable improvement in skin laxity scoring. Clinical studies report an 87% patient satisfaction rate.2

Results Timeline

3 months post-treatmentDeeper collagen remodeling complete
Results duration12–18 months typical
Sessions needed3–4 sessions, spaced 4–6 weeks apart
Downtime2–3 days redness and swelling

Cost Breakdown

A single session ranges from $600–$4,000 depending on treatment area and provider. A full face series of 3 sessions typically runs $1,800–$3,500. According to patient reviews, the average treatment cost is $1,870.3 Most providers offer package pricing that reduces the per-session cost by 10–20%.

The Risk No One Talks About

Morpheus8 reaches 8mm — deep enough to affect fat tissue. When settings are too aggressive on the face, this can cause permanent fat atrophy (volume loss). Medical experts recommend using only 1–2mm depth on facial areas and reserving deeper settings for body treatments.4 Provider skill matters enormously with this device.

Is It Worth It?

For patients with mild-to-moderate skin laxity, acne scarring, or texture concerns who want non-surgical results — yes, for most. For deep wrinkles or significant volume loss, fillers or fat transfer may be more effective. Always consult a licensed provider who is experienced specifically with RF microneedling before booking.

Chemical Peel Guide: Which Type Is Right for Your Skin?

Seeking Aesthetics · Utah · 2026 · 4 min read
For informational purposes only. This article does not constitute medical advice. Always consult a licensed medical professional before pursuing any aesthetic treatment. Seeking Aesthetics is not a healthcare provider.

A light peel is a lunch break. A deep peel is two weeks of recovery. Most patients don't know which type they're getting — or which one their skin actually needs. Here's the complete breakdown.

The Three Types — Side by Side

TypeDepthCommon AcidsDowntimeBest ForCost Range
Light (Superficial)Epidermis onlyGlycolic, Lactic, Salicylic1–3 daysMild texture, dullness, maintenance$150–$400
MediumPapillary dermisTCA 35%, Jessner's7–14 daysSun damage, moderate wrinkles, pigmentation$600–$1,200

Light Peels — The Maintenance Peel

Superficial peels use alpha-hydroxy acids (AHAs) like glycolic acid or beta-hydroxy acids (BHAs) like salicylic acid to exfoliate only the top layer of skin.1 Most patients experience mild redness for 1–3 days. No real downtime. Best in a series of 6–8 treatments, 2–3 weeks apart. Ideal for glow, mild acne, and skin maintenance.

Medium Peels — The Real Work Horse

TCA (trichloroacetic acid) peels penetrate deeper, reaching the upper layers of the dermis. Recovery is 7–14 days of significant peeling and redness.2 Results are more dramatic: visible reduction in sun damage, pigmentation, and moderate wrinkles. Requires medical supervision in most states.

Deep Peels — The Most Dramatic Option

Phenol peels penetrate deepest and deliver the most dramatic results — but require anesthesia, 2–8 weeks of recovery, and carry the highest risk. Most patients only need one in a lifetime. Not appropriate for darker skin tones due to risk of pigmentation changes. Always performed by a physician.3

The One Thing to Do Before Any Peel

Avoid retinoids, AHAs, and BHAs for at least 5–7 days before any peel — they thin the skin and increase sensitivity. Never get a medium or deep peel within 2–3 weeks of significant sun exposure. Your skin will be highly vulnerable to UV damage during the healing period.

How to Compare MedSpa Pricing Without Getting Confused

Seeking Aesthetics · Utah · 2026 · 4 min read
For informational purposes only. This article does not constitute medical advice. Always consult a licensed medical professional before pursuing any aesthetic treatment. Seeking Aesthetics is not a healthcare provider.

MedSpa pricing is intentionally complicated. Per unit, per area, per syringe, membership pricing, package deals — it's designed to make comparison shopping nearly impossible. Here's how to see through it.

The Two Pricing Models — and Why They Don't Compare

Pricing ModelWhat It MeansRed Flag?
Per unit (Botox)You pay for each unit injected. Most transparent.No — preferred
Per area (Botox)Flat fee per treatment zone. You don't know how many units.Sometimes — ask how many units are included
Per syringe (filler)Standard for fillers. 1ml per syringe is typical.No — this is normal
Membership pricingLow per-unit rate but requires $150–$300/month membership.Run the math — often costs more
Package / bundle pricingDiscounted rate for pre-purchasing sessions.Proceed with caution — verify refund policy

The Membership Math You Need to Do

A MedSpa advertises Botox at $9/unit — but only for members who pay $199/month. If you get Botox every 4 months at 30 units, you'd spend $270 on Botox plus $796 on membership = $1,066/year. The same 90 units at $13/unit without membership = $1,170/year. The difference is only $104 — and you're not locked into a monthly fee.

Always calculate your annual cost — not just the per-unit rate.

The Questions to Ask Before Booking

For Botox: How many units will you use? What is the per-unit price? What brand are you using (Botox, Dysport, Xeomin, Daxxify)?
For filler: How many syringes? What product? What is your refund or dissolve policy if I don't like the result?
For any treatment: What does the price include — numbing, follow-up, touch-ups?

The Red Flags

"Call for pricing." No one who is transparent about pricing hides it behind a phone call. Membership required to see real prices. A quote that only gives you a total without breaking down units or volume. Prices significantly below market rate — cheapest Botox in Utah carries a real risk.

GLP-1 & Skin Laxity: What to Do After Weight Loss

Seeking Aesthetics · Utah · 2026 · 5 min read
For informational purposes only. This article does not constitute medical advice. Always consult a licensed medical professional before pursuing any aesthetic treatment. Seeking Aesthetics is not a healthcare provider.

GLP-1 medications like Ozempic, Wegovy, and Mounjaro are changing bodies across Utah — and the results are visible. But rapid weight loss comes with an aesthetic cost that almost no one talks about before they start: skin laxity. Here's what actually helps.

Why GLP-1 Affects Your Skin

When you lose weight rapidly, fat cells shrink faster than skin can contract. Collagen and elastin — the proteins that keep skin firm — don't rebuild overnight. The result is loose, crepey skin, especially in the face, neck, décolletage, abdomen, and upper arms. The faster the weight loss, the more pronounced the laxity.

What Treatment Helps — and When

TreatmentWhat It AddressesWhen to Start
Morpheus8 RFSkin tightening, collagen rebuildingOnce weight has stabilized (3+ months)
Sculptra / BiostimulatorsVolume replacement, collagen stimulationAfter weight stabilizes
Dermal fillersFacial volume loss ("Ozempic face")After weight stabilizes
Emsculpt / EmSellaMuscle building, body contouringDuring active weight loss OK
CoolSculptingStubborn fat pockets remainingAfter weight stabilizes
IV therapy / nutritionSkin health support, hydrationAnytime

The Timing Rule Most Patients Get Wrong

Starting aesthetic treatments while still actively losing weight is a mistake. If your weight fluctuates by more than 10–15 lbs after treatment, filler placement may look different, skin tightening results may not hold, and you'll likely need to retreat. Wait until your weight has been stable for at least 3 months before investing in tightening or volume treatments.

"Ozempic Face" — What It Is and What Helps

Rapid facial fat loss causes a hollowed, gaunt appearance — colloquially called "Ozempic face." The most effective treatment is collagen biostimulators (Sculptra, Radiesse) or hyaluronic acid fillers in the cheeks, temples, and under the eyes. Results look natural and restore the volume that was lost — without reversing the weight loss itself.

Sun, saltwater, heat, and chlorine are the enemies of fresh aesthetic treatments. Getting the timing wrong means bruising that peaks on day 3 — which happens to be your first day on the beach. Here's the complete pre-vacation treatment timeline.

TreatmentBook This Far in AdvanceWhy
Laser hair removal (series)4–6 weeks minimumMultiple sessions required; sun sensitivity after each

What to Avoid Right Before You Leave

Do not get any of the following within 7 days of a beach vacation: medium or deep chemical peels, laser resurfacing, RF microneedling, or your first filler treatment. Freshly treated skin is highly vulnerable to hyperpigmentation when exposed to UV rays.1

HydraFacial 3–5 days before is ideal — it deeply hydrates your skin for travel and has zero downtime. Botox and Dysport can be done 2 weeks out safely for experienced patients. Injectables: avoid sun for 24–48 hours and use SPF 30+ if you must be outside.2

When in doubt, go earlier. It's always better to have a treatment settled and healed than to be dealing with peeling skin or filler bruising poolside. Plan backward from your departure date, not forward from when you remember to book.

What DOPL Verification Means — and Why It Protects You

Seeking Aesthetics · Utah · 2026 · 3 min read
For informational purposes only. This article does not constitute medical advice. Always consult a licensed medical professional before pursuing any aesthetic treatment. Seeking Aesthetics is not a healthcare provider.

Most patients in Utah have never heard of DOPL — and most MedSpas don't bring it up. But in a state where anyone can rent a suite and call themselves an aesthetic injector, a DOPL verification is the only objective proof that your provider is actually licensed to treat you.

What DOPL Is

DOPL — the Utah Division of Professional Licensing — is a division of the Utah Department of Commerce that issues and regulates professional licenses across all healthcare and licensed professions in Utah.1 Every licensed physician, nurse, nurse practitioner, and physician assistant in Utah has an active record in the DOPL database.

What DOPL Verification Confirms

What It ConfirmsWhy It Matters
The provider has an active Utah licenseAn expired license means they're practicing illegally
The license type (RN, NP, MD, PA)Only certain license types can legally administer injectables
No disciplinary actions on recordComplaints, suspensions, and revocations appear here
License expiration dateConfirms the license is current — not expired

What DOPL Does Not Tell You

DOPL confirms a license exists — it does not confirm aesthetic-specific training, years of injection experience, or quality of results. A license is the floor, not the ceiling. You still need to review a provider's before-and-after portfolio and ask about their aesthetic training specifically.

How Seeking Aesthetics Uses DOPL

Before any provider goes live on Seeking Aesthetics, we verify their DOPL license manually at dopl.utah.gov. Their license number is displayed on their profile tile. If their license expires or a disciplinary action is filed, they are removed from the platform. This is the standard we set — and it's non-negotiable.

You can also verify any Utah provider yourself — not just those on our platform — at secure.utah.gov/llv/search in under 60 seconds.
